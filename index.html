About this project

Built by Mikey Wessman, who is interested in understanding human behavior at every level. This is a tool, not an argument. Poke around.

What you're looking at

Every General Conference talk given by the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints from 1971 through 2025 — 4,001 talks in total — scraped from churchofjesuschrist.org and made searchable by word, gender, session, year, and speaker.

Where the data came from

  • Talks: scraped directly from the church's public General Conference archive. Each talk's full text, speaker, year, session, and session type (general / women's / priesthood).
  • Speaker genders: tagged in speaker_genders.csv. The overwhelming majority were straightforward (the archive lists callings and pronouns). Ambiguous cases were reviewed manually.
  • Session types: pulled from the archive's own session labels.
  • Code and raw data: github.com/MikWess/women-at-the-pulpit. Clone it, run the scripts, verify anything you want.

Methodology notes

  • Word matching. The word-frequency tool uses word-boundary matching — searching "mother" finds "mother" but not "grandmother" or "mothers". Toggle + synonyms to include morphological variants and related terms (curated, not algorithmic).
  • "Per 10k words" is the fairest metric for comparing groups of different sizes. Women have given roughly 6% of talks; raw counts will always favor men. Rate-per-10k-words strips that away.
  • Statistical significance. Gender comparisons use a two-proportion z-test on word-level rates. p < 0.05 is marked as significant.
  • "Surprising words" are ranked by absolute difference in per-10k rates (for gender gap) or by linear regression slope across years (for time trend). Words appearing fewer than 200 times corpus-wide are excluded to reduce noise.
  • "Topics" are not machine-learned clusters — they're 18 curated keyword sets (e.g. the "Priesthood Authority" topic watches for phrases like aaronic priesthood, melchizedek priesthood, priesthood keys). For each topic, a talk's score is the rate of those keywords per 10,000 words. A talk isn't forced into a single topic — the Topics tab instead ranks talks by how densely each topic appears and shows the top-scoring ones. This avoids the problem of keyword overlap (Jesus Christ and Atonement both mention "savior") while still surfacing which speakers lean into which themes.

What this tool doesn't account for

  • Women's-session talks are included in "all" by default. Filter to "general session" only if you want to compare apples-to-apples on the biggest stage.
  • Before 1978, women's talks were rare in General Conference specifically. The data reflects that — the rate of women speakers climbs sharply in the 1980s onward.
  • Topic isn't labeled. Word frequency is a proxy for topic, not a substitute. A word being frequent in women's talks doesn't prove women "care more" about it — it could also be a function of what women have been asked to speak on.
  • Quoting the scriptures or each other inflates certain words across everyone.
  • Procedural / facilitation talks are included. Sustaining of officers, statistical reports, conference-opening and conference-closing remarks, and other short administrative items are counted as "talks" alongside sermons. They're almost always given by men in senior callings, and they skew the talk-length distribution toward shorter lengths and inflate a handful of top male speakers' talk counts. There's no clean automated way to strip these out — a future version of this tool may flag them explicitly.
